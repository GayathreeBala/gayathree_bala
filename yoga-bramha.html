Bramhari Pranayama




Bhramari Pranayama, also known as Humming Bee Breath, is a calming breathing practice that soothes the nervous system and helps to connect us with our truest inner nature. Bhramari is the Sanskrit word for “bee,” and this pranayama is so named because of the humming sound produced at the back of the throat during the practice—like the gentle humming of a bee.

Benefits of Doing Bramhari Pranayama


  • Calms and quiets the mind
  • Releases cerebral tension
  • Stimulates the pineal and pituitary glands, supporting their proper functioning
  • Soothes the nerves
  • Stress and emotional regulation. Pranayama’s ability to improve mindfulness has also been found to lower stress and aggression among students taking particularly stressful exams. Pranayama’s focus on breathing and relaxation may alter the levels of stress molecules.
  • Strengthens and improves the voice
  • Supports the healing of bodily tissues
  • Induces sound sleep

    • Practicing Bramhari Pranayama


  • Sit in a quiet and well ventilated corner and close your eyes.
  • Place your index fingers on your ears right at the cartilage.
  • Breathe in and while breathing out press the cartilage with your fingers. Keep the cartilage pressed while making a loud humming sound like a bee.

    • Practicing Bhramari pranayama gives you instant way to relieve tension, anger and anxiety. People suffering from hypertension should practice this breathing technique as it calms down the agitated mind. It helps mitigate migraines. It is also effective as it improves concentration and memory and builds confidence. Bhramari Pranayama also helps in reducing blood pressure.



    How to make the practice effective?


  • The preferable time to do Bhramari Pranayama is early in the morning.
  • One round of Brahmari Pranayama mudra might be repeated 5-10 times. The longer you can exhale with a humming sound, the more relaxed you get.
  • Practice this technique at home on an empty stomach.




    • Who shouldn't Perform?


    Bhramari should not be practiced by pregnant or menstruating women. It is also contraindicated for individuals with extremely high blood pressure, epilepsy, chest pain, or an active ear infection. Bhramari should not be practiced in a supine position (lying down).




    Best Time to Practice

    Bhramari (as with most pranayamas) is best practiced on an empty stomach. While it can be practiced at any time of day, bhramari is particularly potent in the early morning and late at night—when there are fewer distracting noises and our inner perception is most acute.
